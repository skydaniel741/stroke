# Dryland exercises

Swim-relevant dryland only — no generic gym programming pasted in from a
bodybuilding split. Three mandatory categories per session (adjust duration
split by focus, but don't drop a category entirely unless the load guardrail
or an injury modification says to):

1. **Shoulder / rotator cuff health** — the highest-yield category for a
   competitive swimmer. Most sessions should touch this even on a light day.
2. **Core / rotation** — supports catch, body roll, and streamline; swimming
   is a rotational sport, not a sagittal-plane one.
3. **Explosive / plyometric** — starts and turns are the only truly explosive
   moments in a swim race; dryland is where that power gets built, the pool
   can't build it directly.

A fourth category, **arms (elbow flexion/extension)**, is optional accessory
work — add it when a session has time beyond the three mandatory categories,
don't let it crowd out cuff/core/plyo work.

**⚠ FLAGGED FOR VERIFICATION** throughout this file: exact set/rep/tempo
prescriptions for cuff and plyo work are a reasonable S&C starting point, not
a validated protocol. Check against current AustSwim / ASCA guidance before
this goes live to users.

## Equipment → what it unlocks

Most competitive swimmers do **not** have a home gym. Ask what they actually
have (pool deck, home, or a gym) before prescribing. Never prescribe
equipment they don't have.

| Equipment | Patterns it unlocks |
|---|---|
| Bodyweight only | Planks/anti-rotation holds, push-ups, Y-T-W raises (no load), bodyweight squats/lunges, pool-deck plyo (broad jumps, box-free bounds) |
| Resistance bands (light + medium) | External/internal rotation, face pulls, pull-aparts, Pallof press, banded good mornings, banded lateral walks — the single most useful piece of kit for cuff work and the cheapest to ask a swimmer to buy |
| Light dumbbells (1-5 kg) | Cuban press, prone Y-T-W with load, scaption raises, single-arm carries, weighted core |
| Medicine ball (2-4 kg) | Rotational throws, slam, chest pass — power/core combo, low technical barrier |
| Box or stable platform | Box jumps, depth drops (progression only, see plyo notes), step-ups |
| Full gym (rack, cable stack) | Cable face pulls, cable external rotation, TRX/rings for scap stability, loaded squats/hinges as a base for power work — most competitive swimmers training seriously will have at least gym access even without a home setup |
| Pool deck / starting blocks (coach-supervised only) | Actual start/turn practice — not this skill's job to prescribe unsupervised block work; note it as the coach's domain, dryland here is the land-based power that *feeds* that skill |

If equipment is sparse (bodyweight + bands only, very common), that's still
enough for a complete session — don't apologize for it or wait for a gym.

## 1. Shoulder / rotator cuff health

Rationale: swimmers accumulate very high shoulder volume with a repetitive,
internally-rotated pattern (especially freestyle/butterfly). Cuff and scap
stabilizer work is injury *prevention*, not accessory fluff — treat it as
core to every session, not something to cut when short on time.

**⚠ VERIFY exact prescriptions below** — general S&C consensus, not a cited
swim-specific protocol:

| Exercise | Sets × reps | Equipment | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Prone Y-T-W raises | 2-3 × 10-12 each position | Bodyweight or light DB | Scap control focus, slow tempo, no shrugging |
| Band external rotation (elbow at side) | 3 × 12-15 | Light band | Classic cuff-strengthening staple; keep elbow pinned to ribs |
| Band internal rotation | 2-3 × 12-15 | Light band | Often under-trained relative to external rotation — include both |
| Cuban press | 2-3 × 8-10 | Light DB or band | Combines upright row + external rotation + press; technical, cue slow and controlled |
| Scaption raises (thumbs-up, ~30° forward of frontal plane) | 2-3 × 10-12 | Light DB or band | Lower impingement risk than straight lateral raise |
| Face pulls / band pull-aparts | 2-3 × 15 | Band or cable | Rear delt + scap retraction, high volume tolerated well |
| Wall slides / scapular push-ups | 2 × 10-12 | Bodyweight | Scap stability and serratus anterior — good warm-up or activation piece |

**Dosage note (⚠ verify):** for *endurance/health* purposes (not building
maximal cuff strength), higher rep ranges (12-15+) with light load are
generally favored over heavy low-rep work, since the cuff's job is
stabilization through thousands of repetitive strokes, not maximal force
production. Confirm this rep-range reasoning against current literature.

## 2. Core / rotation

Supports the body roll and streamline position, not a six-pack aesthetic
goal. Favor anti-rotation and controlled-rotation work over crunches.

| Exercise | Sets × reps/time | Equipment | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Plank (front + side) | 2-3 × 30-45s | Bodyweight | Baseline anti-extension/anti-lateral-flexion |
| Pallof press | 2-3 × 10-12 each side | Band or cable | Anti-rotation, directly relevant to maintaining a stable core while the arms rotate through the stroke |
| Dead bug | 2-3 × 8-10 each side | Bodyweight | Coordination + anti-extension, good for younger/newer athletes |
| Med ball rotational throw | 2-3 × 6-8 each side | Med ball + wall | Controlled rotational *power*, not just stability — bridges core and explosive categories |
| Russian twist (controlled, not fast/flailing) | 2-3 × 12-16 | Bodyweight or light med ball | Rotational strength through range; cue control over speed |
| Hollow body hold | 2-3 × 20-30s | Bodyweight | Streamline-position core endurance — directly transferable to streamline off the wall |

## 3. Explosive / plyometric (starts and turns)

Purpose: build the leg power and rate of force development that starts and
wall push-offs demand. This is the category most sensitive to overload — see
the load guardrail in
[load-management.md](load-management.md) before prescribing on a
high-pool-load day.

**⚠ VERIFY progression and volume caps below** — this is a general
plyometric progression principle, not a swim-specific validated protocol,
and caps should scale with age and training age (a 13-year-old age-grouper
and an adult senior swimmer should not get the same volume).

Progression order (don't skip ahead):

1. **Landing mechanics first** — box step-offs / controlled landings before
   any jumping. If an athlete hasn't done plyo work before, spend the first
   session or two here regardless of level.
2. **Bilateral, low-amplitude** — squat jumps, broad jumps (short distance),
   ankle hops.
3. **Bilateral, higher-amplitude** — box jumps (moderate height, stick the
   landing), depth-jump-to-broad-jump.
4. **Unilateral** — single-leg bounds, lateral bounds — closer to the
   asymmetric push-off pattern of a real start/turn, introduce once bilateral
   work is solid.
5. **Depth drops / reactive work** — most advanced, highest joint stress;
   reserve for experienced athletes with clean landing mechanics, and only a
   handful of reps.

| Exercise | Sets × reps | Level | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Box step-offs (land soft, stick it) | 2-3 × 5 | Beginner / first plyo sessions | Landing mechanics, not power output yet |
| Squat jumps | 3 × 5-6 | All levels | Full recovery between reps — this is a power exercise, not conditioning; don't turn it into a cardio circuit |
| Broad jumps | 3 × 4-5 | All levels | Emphasize horizontal power, relevant to the push off the wall |
| Box jumps (moderate height) | 3 × 5 | Intermediate+ | Height is secondary to landing quality — don't chase max height |
| Single-leg bounds | 2-3 × 4-5 each side | Intermediate+ | Closer to true start/turn asymmetry |
| Depth jumps | 2-3 × 3-5 | Advanced, clean landing mechanics only | Highest stress — skip for anyone under a certain training age or with any lower-limb injury history (⚠ verify age/training-age cutoff) |

**Volume/frequency guardrail (⚠ verify):** true plyometric work 1-2×/week is
a reasonable starting cap for most swimmers, given the pool itself already
provides high total lower-body and shoulder loading. Don't stack plyo on the
same day as the hardest water session of the week unless the combined-load
check in [load-management.md](load-management.md) clears it.

## 4. Arms (elbow flexion/extension) — optional accessory

Rationale: the upper-arm/forearm bones are the levers the arm muscles pull
on. Two muscle groups matter — **elbow extensors** (triceps brachii,
assisted by the anconeus) straighten the arm; **elbow flexors** (biceps
brachii and brachialis, assisted by the brachioradialis) bend it. In
freestyle, backstroke, and butterfly the elbow bends 30-90° through the catch
(flexors) then straightens through the second half of the pull (extensors).
Breaststroke runs the opposite order: extended early in the pull, flexors
bend the elbow through the insweep, extensors straighten it again for
recovery.

Because extensors do more work than flexors across all four strokes, split
roughly **2 triceps exercises for every 1 biceps exercise**, and place these
single-joint isolation moves near the **end** of a session so a small muscle
group isn't pre-fatigued before compound lifts. Before any exercise here, cue
setting the shoulder blades back and down (not shrugged up — swimmers
already tend to have overdeveloped upper traps) and bracing the core.

**⚠ VERIFY exact prescriptions below** — general S&C consensus for hypertrophy/
strength-endurance rep ranges, not a cited swim-specific protocol:

| Exercise | Sets × reps | Equipment | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Barbell bicep curl | 3 × 8-12 | Barbell | Baseline elbow-flexion strength builder; don't lean back to cheat the curl |
| Dumbbell bicep curl | 2-3 × 10-12 each arm | Dumbbells | Rotate the wrist to palm-in at the top — mirrors the forearm turn late in the breaststroke pull; alternating arms surfaces left/right imbalances |
| Concentration curl | 2-3 × 10-12 each arm | Dumbbell + bench | Elbow braced against inner thigh isolates the flexors cleanly; use if curls are turning into a swinging mess |
| Tate press | 2-3 × 10-12 | Dumbbells + bench | Targets the lateral triceps head specifically, under-hit by basic pressing; keep the dumbbells touching, don't let them drop onto the chest |
| Medicine ball chest pass | 3 × 6-8 | Med ball (2.5-5 kg) + partner or wall | Explosive, not slow/controlled — builds power; catch-and-redirect mirrors absorbing momentum on open turns in fly/breaststroke |
| Standing double-arm triceps pushdown | 3 × 10-15 | Cable machine | Mirrors the final part of the underwater pull off the start and wall turns (especially breaststroke); keep the torso upright, elbows pinned to sides |
| Dumbbell kickback | 2-3 × 10-12 each arm | Dumbbell + bench | Drives the last 90° of extension — the range that finishes propulsion in freestyle, fly, and backstroke; pause at full extension, keep head in line with spine |
| Close-grip push-up | 2-3 × 8-15 | Bodyweight | Pool-deck friendly; closed-chain shoulder loading recruits more stabilizers than open-chain moves; drop to knees if a straight-body line can't be held; skip for younger swimmers still building shoulder strength or anyone with shoulder pain history (don't drop below neutral shoulder height) |
| Close-grip bench press | 3 × 8-10 | Barbell + bench | Loadable alternative to close-grip push-up for swimmers not yet strong enough for good push-up form, or advanced swimmers whose triceps outgrew bodyweight work; let elbows flare ~45°; widen grip if wrist pain occurs; those with shoulder pain/history shouldn't let elbows dip below bench level |

## Conflicting-goals note

If an athlete wants heavy plyometric progression on a schedule that only
allows dryland twice a week for 15-20 min, say so — that's enough time for
cuff + core maintenance, not enough for a real plyo progression. Offer the
honest tradeoff rather than cramming all three categories into a rushed
session every time.
